Tree of the Day
Banyan Tree
Ficus benghalensis

India's national tree, the Banyan is a strangler fig that begins life as a seed in another tree's canopy. Its iconic aerial roots descend to the ground, forming new trunks that allow a single tree to spread over several hectares. The Great Banyan in Kolkata's Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Botanical Garden covers over 3.5 acres — making it the widest tree in the world.

Interesting Facts

What makes them extraordinary

🌳
Peepal · Ficus religiosa
The only tree that exhales oxygen 24 hours a day

Unlike most plants that release CO₂ at night, the sacred Peepal performs Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM), enabling continuous oxygen production. This is why ancient scriptures forbade sleeping under it at night — the O₂ surge was believed to attract spirits.

🐯
Bengal Tiger · Panthera tigris tigris
Each tiger's stripes are as unique as a human fingerprint

No two tigers share the same stripe pattern. Wildlife conservationists use stripe photography — called "camera trap identification" — to track individuals through dense forests without disturbing them, building population databases for Project Tiger reserves across India.

🎋
Bamboo · Bambusoideae
A grass that outgrows any tree — up to 91 cm in one day

Technically a grass, certain bamboo species recorded in India's Northeast grow up to 91 cm in 24 hours. The Mautak bamboo of Mizoram flowers only once every 48 years — and when it does, it triggers a famine-level rat population explosion known as "Mautam."

🐘
Asian Elephant · Elephas maximus
Elephants communicate through seismic vibrations in the ground

Asian elephants produce low-frequency rumbles — some inaudible to humans — that travel through the earth as seismic waves. Herds can detect these vibrations through their sensitive feet and trunks from over 30 kilometres away, enabling long-distance communication across forest corridors.

🌴
Areca Palm · Areca catechu
A palm that sustains 600 million people's daily ritual

The areca nut (supari) is chewed daily by an estimated 600 million people globally — making it the fourth most widely consumed psychoactive substance after tobacco, alcohol and caffeine. Every part of the tree, from sheaths to fronds, finds traditional use across South and Southeast Asia.

🦜
Indian Peacock · Pavo cristatus
The train isn't a tail — and it's controlled by UV vision

The peacock's spectacular "tail" is actually its upper tail covert feathers. Peahens choose mates partly based on UV reflectance patterns invisible to human eyes. The quivering display produces low-frequency sound waves (infrasound) below 100 Hz that researchers believe also attract females from considerable distances.
