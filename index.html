Species of the Day
Tree of the Day
Banyan Tree
Ficus benghalensis

India's national tree, the Banyan begins life as a tiny seed in another tree's canopy. Its aerial roots descend to the ground, forming new trunks that can let one single tree expand into a forest. The Great Banyan in Kolkata covers 3.5 acres — the widest tree on Earth.

Extraordinary Facts

What makes them unforgettable

🌳
Peepal · Ficus religiosa
The only tree that exhales oxygen 24 hours a day

Unlike most plants that release CO₂ at night, the sacred Peepal performs Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM), enabling continuous oxygen production — making it uniquely sacred in Indian tradition.

🐯
Bengal Tiger · Panthera tigris tigris
Each tiger's stripes are as unique as a fingerprint

No two tigers share the same stripe pattern. Camera-trap identification using stripe photography now underpins India's entire Project Tiger census — tracking individuals across thousands of square kilometres.

🎋
Bamboo · Bambusoideae
A grass that outgrows any tree — 91 cm in one day

Technically a grass, bamboo is the Earth's fastest growing plant. The Mizo "Mautam" — when bamboo flowers every 48 years — triggers a rat population explosion that has caused historical famines in Mizoram.

🐘
Asian Elephant · Elephas maximus
Elephants feel seismic vibrations from 30 kilometres away

Asian elephants produce low-frequency rumbles that travel through the earth as seismic waves, detectable by other elephants through their sensitive feet and trunks from over 30 km away.

🌴
Areca Palm · Areca catechu
A palm that sustains 600 million people's daily ritual

The areca nut (supari) is the world's fourth most widely consumed psychoactive substance. Every part of the tree — from sheaths used as plates to leaves used as packaging — has traditional uses across South Asia.

🦚
Indian Peacock · Pavo cristatus
The train isn't a tail — it's controlled by UV vision

The peacock's spectacular display is upper tail covert feathers, not a true tail. Peahens select mates based on UV reflectance invisible to humans. The quivering display also produces infrasound below 100 Hz to attract females from afar.
